Re-emergance of Whooping Cough

Welcome to Vizeases

In the past 5-10 years, diseases which were previously suppressed have begun to re-emerge. Multiple explanations have been proposed, ranging from a surge in certain bug populations (Lyme disease) to the anti-vaccine movement.

Pertussis, or whooping cough, is one of these re-emerging diseases. Whooping cough is a very contagious bacterial disease that can be spread through saliva and mucus. It causes a severe cough, which is often followed by a "whooping" inhale. In 2010, California experienced nearly 10,000 reported whooping cough cases, the highest number it had seen 70 years. In 2014, the national level of whooping cough cases reached more than 48,000, which was higher than any count for the previous 6 decades. Multiple causes for this rise have been proposed, many of which are related to vaccine, such as a descrease in vaccine effectiveness, vaccinated people acting as carriers, or unvaccinated children due to the anti-vaccine movement.

We seek to introduce and explore Whooping Cough re-emergence through our system. Our system uses records of whooping cough cases from Project Tycho data. Project Tycho consists of a large dataset containing all weekly surveillance reports of nationally notifiable diseases for all US cities and states published since 1888. Data is missing around the 1960s. According to the Project Tycho members, this is because the period from 1956-1974 had such a low number of cases of whooping cough that they did not include this data. Keep in mind that this data is only missing because the number of cases was so low.
